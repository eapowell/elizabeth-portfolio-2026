Overview

Building a media organization from the ground up.

When I stepped into the Executive News Director role at The UVU Review, I inherited editorial and operational authority for UVU's student-run media organization. I owned the roadmap for web, broadcast, podcast, social, and video simultaneously — and had to build the team to execute it.

Over two years, I treated the publication like a product: recruiting and onboarding talent, designing workflows, tracking metrics, iterating on systems, and making real-time decisions under pressure. The result was a publication that reached 338K readers, published 414+ pieces of content across 9 platforms, and operated with the discipline of a professional newsroom.

The Challenge

How do you build a team that didn't exist yet?

The biggest challenge wasn't editorial — it was organizational. I needed to recruit, interview, and onboard dozens of contributors across editorial, design, video, broadcast, social, advertising, and web. Most had never worked in a newsroom. Many were doing it alongside full course loads and jobs.

Beyond hiring, I needed systems that would actually stick. Student organizations have constant turnover — people graduate, change majors, get busy. Whatever I built had to survive past my own tenure.

Approach

Treat internal tooling as a product problem.

One of the hardest parts of scaling a student team is getting people to actually follow workflows. I designed a creator workflow adoption system and iterated on it like a product: started with Google Sheets, moved to Microsoft Tasks, then added an incentive layer — weekly drawings, public kudos — that drove consistent adoption across all teams.

I also rebuilt the website using Claude Code, applying prompt engineering and context management to accelerate technical output. The new site had faster load times, improved search, team bio pages, and even games as an engagement driver.

Every decision was driven by data — platform analytics and direct audience research. I maintained active relationships with readers, advertisers, and outside mentors, conducting surveys and direct outreach to understand what the audience actually wanted.

Under Pressure

Real-time decisions when it matters.

During a campus crisis, I made real-time product and editorial decisions while managing inbound media requests from the New York Times and NBC. The publication was independent from university administration — meaning every call was mine to make.

That experience reinforced something I already believed: the best product leaders are comfortable with ambiguity and fast-changing priorities. You can't plan for everything. But you can build systems and teams that respond well under pressure.

Reflection

Leadership is a design problem.

Running The UVU Review taught me that building a team is a design problem. You're designing onboarding flows, feedback loops, incentive structures, and communication rhythms — and if any of them break, the whole system slows down.

It also taught me the value of treating every process as something you can iterate on. The workflow system went through three major versions. The editorial calendar evolved constantly. Nothing was precious — what mattered was whether it worked for the people using it.
