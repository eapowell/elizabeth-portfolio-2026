Overview

Teaching history through design and storytelling.

How did the ideas of a few Scottish thinkers in the 1700s shape the Declaration of Independence, the founding of the United States, and the culture of innovation that defines America today? That was the question at the heart of this project — and we had to answer it in a way that 6th graders would actually care about.

I led a team of 5 other designers through nearly a year of research and pre-production, then traveled to the U.K. for 10 days to film, edit, and publish a 3-episode documentary series on location. But the documentary was just one piece — we shipped an entire multi-platform learning experience.

The Challenge

Make 300-year-old history feel alive for 11-year-olds.

The Scottish Enlightenment isn't exactly a topic that grabs a room of 6th graders. Adam Smith, David Hume, the philosophical underpinnings of modern democracy — these are abstract concepts even adults struggle with. We needed to make it tangible, visual, and fun.

On top of the creative challenge, we had real production constraints: film 3 episodes in 10 days, edit and publish while still on location, and deliver supplementary materials for 160 students across 3 classrooms — all while coordinating a team of 6 across an unfamiliar country.

Approach

Design every touchpoint, not just the film.

From the start, we knew the documentary alone wouldn't be enough. 6th graders need multiple ways into a story — watching, reading, touching, exploring. So we designed a complete multi-platform experience around the content.

We created Haggis, an animated unicorn character (Scotland's national animal) who served as the guide across all materials — tying the episodes, website, AR books, and activity books into a cohesive experience that felt playful rather than academic.

The team spent months on research and pre-production — studying the historical figures, scouting filming locations remotely, designing the print materials, and building the website framework before we ever stepped on a plane.

On Location

10 days to film, edit, and publish.

Once in Scotland, every day was tightly planned. We filmed across multiple historic locations, conducted interviews, captured 360 photos, and began editing episodes in the evenings. The goal was to leave the U.K. with finished, published content — not raw footage to sort through later.

Leading a team of 6 in this environment meant constant coordination — managing schedules, reassigning roles on the fly, and making creative decisions in real time. It was the most compressed production timeline I've ever worked on, and it taught me how to ship under extreme constraints.

Reflection

Design is how you make people care.

This project proved to me that great design isn't about making things look good — it's about making people care about things they wouldn't otherwise pay attention to. The Scottish Enlightenment is objectively important, but it takes thoughtful design to make an 11-year-old feel that.

It also taught me how to lead under pressure. When you're in a foreign country with a tight deadline and a team counting on you, there's no room for indecision. You plan deeply, then you execute and adapt. That rhythm — research, plan, ship, iterate — is the same one I bring to product work today.
