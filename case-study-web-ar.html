Overview

Bringing the experience out of the venue.

The Rift's core product is an in-venue AR experience — but that means you have to physically show up to know what it feels like. The Web AR Mini Quest solves that problem by giving potential customers a taste of the experience directly in their phone's browser.

No app store. No download. No friction. Just open the link on your phone and start an interactive AR quest wherever you are.

The Challenge

How do you sell an experience people can't imagine?

AR experiences are hard to market. Screenshots and videos don't capture the magic of interacting with digital objects in physical space. And asking someone to commit to a venue visit (plus booking, travel, and cost) based on marketing alone is a big ask.

We needed a way to lower the barrier to entry — to let people feel the product before committing to it. The challenge was delivering something genuinely immersive through just a web browser, without the control of a dedicated app or venue hardware.

Approach

Design for the first 10 seconds.

The Web AR experience had to hook people immediately. There's no onboarding patience for a free browser experience — if it doesn't feel magical in the first few seconds, people close the tab.

I focused the product design on three principles: instant start (camera permission is the only gate), visual reward in under 5 seconds (something appears in your space right away), and a clear path to the full venue experience (the CTA to book).

The mini quest is deliberately short — it's a teaser, not a replacement. The goal is to create just enough delight that booking the real thing feels like the obvious next step.

Solution

A new acquisition channel built on the web.

The Web AR Mini Quest extends The Rift's reach beyond the venue. It works as a shareable link — embeddable on the website, sendable via text or social, and scannable via QR code at events and partner locations.

This turns every phone into a demo device. Instead of relying on word-of-mouth descriptions of what AR feels like, potential customers can experience a version of it themselves and share it with friends.

The experience ends with a direct path to booking — creating a measurable funnel from "curious" to "booked" that we can track and optimize.

Reflection

The product is the marketing.

The biggest lesson from this project is that the best way to market an experience product is to give people a piece of the experience itself. No amount of copywriting or video can replace the feeling of seeing something appear in your physical space through your phone camera.

Building for the web also forced extreme simplicity. Every decision had to pass the test: "Will this work on a 3-year-old phone on a mediocre connection?" That constraint made the product better.
