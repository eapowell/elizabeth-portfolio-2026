Overview

An AR experience that merges physical space with storytelling.

The Rift is an augmented reality platform built in Unity that merges physical environments, storytelling, and real-time interaction. I've owned the product end-to-end through multiple life cycle phases — defining success metrics, setting the roadmap, and driving execution across engineering, design, and operations.

The product spans the full customer journey: from newsletter and marketing funnel to online booking, the in-venue AR experience itself, and the post-experience review pipeline.

The Challenge

How do you optimize an experience you can't fully see?

AR experiences are hard to measure. Unlike a typical SaaS product, the user journey spans digital touchpoints (website, booking) and physical ones (the venue, the game itself, the staff interaction after). Data was scattered across five different sources — surveys, gameplay telemetry, booking data, an issue tracker, and the CMS — with no unified picture of what was actually driving satisfaction.

At the same time, we had a clear gap between how users felt about the experience (great in-venue survey scores) and what they actually did afterward (very few posted Google reviews). The rating sat at 4.3 — good, but not enough to drive organic growth.

Approach

Data synthesis, then qualitative depth.

I used Claude to simultaneously analyze all five data sources — surveys, gameplay telemetry, booking data, the issue tracker, and the CMS. This surfaced a high-impact finding: groups of 3–4 players rate 4.7 on average vs. 4.0 for pairs, with 0% negative reviews. That insight reshaped how we thought about group size and pricing.

To validate before building, I designed and ran qualitative user tests with mic'd participants, screen recordings, and post-session interviews. The combination of quantitative pattern-finding and qualitative depth gave us confidence to prioritize real changes.

I also set up Google Analytics to track conversion across the entire booking funnel. This revealed that average transaction value (~$35) needed to reach $85 to unlock profitable paid acquisition — a finding that reprioritized the roadmap toward upsell and group-size optimization.

Solution

Closing the gap between delight and action.

The biggest single win was tackling the review pipeline. I identified a 67% drop-off between in-venue 5-star survey responses and posted Google reviews. The problem wasn't satisfaction — it was the handoff.

I defined the problem, designed a new handoff flow, built a 14-slide staff training deck, and created a guided review script. The solution was deliberately low-tech — it had to work with a front desk team in a fast-paced venue environment.

Beyond the review pipeline, I rebuilt the company website and marketing funnels using Claude Code and V0 — expanding our technical output without an engineering team. Every change was tracked, tested, and tied back to the metrics we set.

Reflection

What I learned building at the edge.

The Rift taught me that product management for physical + digital experiences requires a different kind of thinking. You can't A/B test the feeling of walking into a room with AR overlays. But you can instrument everything around it — the booking flow, the group dynamics, the post-experience moment — and use that data to make the core experience better.

Working as a solo PM with board-level responsibility also shaped how I communicate tradeoffs. When you're explaining to investors why group-size optimization matters more than a new feature, you need to be precise about the data and clear about the strategy.
